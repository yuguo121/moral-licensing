# Literature Review: The Morality and Psychology of Earnings Management

## Overview
This document synthesizes current scholarship, primarily from the *Journal of Business Ethics* and related accounting ethics research, regarding how scholars frame and treat earnings management (EM) as a moral and psychological phenomenon. This review focuses on the "gray zone" of financial reporting and the mechanism of moral licensing.

---

## 1. Framing Earnings Management: The "Gray Zone" and Moral Intensity
Modern scholarship has moved beyond a binary "legal vs. illegal" view of earnings management, instead conceptualizing it as a nuanced ethical challenge.

*   **The "Gray Zone":** Researchers like Lapointe-Antunes et al. (2021) describe EM as existing in a "gray zone" between legitimate signaling and opportunistic deception. Because most EM practices operate within the boundaries of GAAP, they are neither completely ethical nor overtly criminal, making them susceptible to subjective psychological interpretation.
*   **Moral Intensity:** The ethical evaluation of EM is driven by its perceived *moral intensity* (Jones, 1991). This includes the magnitude of consequences and social consensus. When managers perceive EM as a "white lie" or a strategic tool to prevent market volatility, the moral intensity is lowered, facilitating its psychological justification.
*   **White Lie Perception:** Financial reporting discretion is often framed by insiders as a "pragmatic response" to market pressures rather than a moral failing. This allows managers to distort financial reality while maintaining a positive moral self-concept.

## 2. The Psychology of the Actor: Moral Licensing and Credits
The application of **Moral Licensing Theory** (Merritt et al., 2010) provides a robust framework for understanding why "ethical" firms might engage in questionable financial reporting.

*   **Moral Bank Account (Credits):** Managers accumulate "moral credits" through substantive prosocial actions (e.g., resource-intensive ESG efforts). These credits function as a psychological currency that can be "spent" to justify subsequent deviations in other domains, such as earnings management.
*   **Moral Credentials (Reframing):** Past virtuous behavior acts as a lens for interpreting current actions. A history of substantive ESG engagement can serve as a "moral credential," allowing managers to reframe earnings management as "protecting the firm" or "managing stakeholder expectations" rather than "cheating."
*   **Sacrifice vs. Transaction:** A critical distinction in the literature is between *moral sacrifice* and *instrumental exchange*. Licensing is most powerful when the initial good deed is perceived as a genuine sacrifice for the greater good (e.g., environmental remediation costs) rather than a quid-pro-quo transaction (e.g., employee training with immediate productivity returns).

## 3. Stakeholder and Insider Perspectives
How EM is judged depends heavily on the *causal attributions* made by observers (Kaplan et al., 2007).

*   **Internal Labor Markets:** Within firms, colleagues judge a manager's reputation based more on their assessment of the manager's *morality* than on the external constraints (like rigid budgets) they faced. 
*   **Culpability and Legitimacy:** For firms in stigmatized or "culpable" industries, prosocial actions are often viewed as "remedial necessity" rather than "extra progress." In these cases, the "commitment frame" suppresses the moral licensing effect, as the good deeds are seen as debt repayment rather than credit accumulation.

1.  **Hypothesis 1 (Direct Effect):** Use the **"Gray Zone"** framing (Lapointe-Antunes et al., 2021) to explain why EM is a primary candidate for moral licensing—it is ambiguous enough to be reframed as a "strategic necessity" after high-cost ESG sacrifice.

3.  **Moderation (Hypothesis 2):** Connect the "Legitimacy Deficit" literature to the **"Commitment Frame"** (Merritt et al., 2010). Explain that in culpable industries, the lack of licensing isn't just about external scrutiny, but an internal psychological shift from "progress" to "identity repair."

## 4. Original Statements and Quotes from JBE

The following statements are extracted directly from peer-reviewed research in the *Journal of Business Ethics*, highlighting the moral complexity of earnings management:

### On the Internal Judgment of Peers
> "Participants' judgments about the target manager's reputation were more strongly associated with morality judgments than with causal attributions... Designers of responsibility accounting systems need to understand the reputational costs managers impose on one another within internal labor markets." 
> — **Kaplan, S. E., McElroy, J. C., & Ravenscroft, S.** (2007)

### On the "Slippery Slope" and Intentionality
> "The seven responding controllers all indicated that our minor violation decision (i.e., giving away merchandising items) represented a very minor infraction, while our major violation (i.e., misreporting of expenses) was serious and represented intentional earnings management."
> — **Rose, A. M., Rose, J. M., & Suh, I.** (2020)

### On the Ethical Environment (Tone at the Top)
> "Executives are willing to engage in misreporting behavior when there is a positive tone set by the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) (kind attitude toward employees and non-aggressive attitude about earnings), regardless of the presence or absence of a slippery slope."
> — **Rose, A. M., Rose, J. M., & Suh, I.** (2020)

### On the Gray Zone of Honesty
> "Most discretionary accounting choices belong to the significant gray zone between signalling and opportunism and are neither completely ethically nor unethically acceptable... individual predispositions are likely to be a significant determinant of the decision to engage in earnings management."
> — **Lapointe-Antunes, P., Veenstra, K., & Brown, K.** (2021)

### On the Rationalization of Acceptability
> "His research focuses on perceptions of acceptability that result from the ability to rationalize earnings management. Brown (2014) examines whether exposure to egregious examples of earnings management lead to rationalization of less egregious forms of earnings management."
> — **Rose, A. M., Rose, J. M., & Suh, I.** (2020), discussing Brown (2014) in JBE context.

### On the Role of Professional Skepticism
> "According to moral licensing theory, the disclosure of a KAM [Key Audit Matter] can reduce the skeptical judgement of auditors. Moral licensing theory states that, under certain circumstances, a person's virtue licenses them to act less than virtuously."
> — **Pinto, I., Morais, A. I., & Quick, R.** (2020) [Note: While published in JIAAT, this explicitly applies JBE-derived moral licensing concepts to the EM context.]

## 5. Detailed Review of Moral Licensing Papers in JBE

This section provides a rigorous breakdown of how moral licensing is researched specifically within the *Journal of Business Ethics*.

### Summary of Focal Relationships and Methods

| Paper | Focal Relationship | Key Arguments | Methodology & Endogeneity Treatment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Zhang & Gong (2024)** | CER & Green Innovation | High performance in Corporate Environmental Responsibility (CER) creates a moral license that reduces subsequent motivation for genuine green innovation. | **Archival Study:** Uses Chinese listed firm data. Employs **Instrumental Variable (IV)** approach and **Propensity Score Matching (PSM)** to address endogeneity. |
| **Koch-Bayram & Biemann (2024)** | Micro-CSR & Private Prosociality | Organizational CSR (ECSR) leads to private moral consistency (more private giving) while ECSIR (irresponsibility) reduces it. | **Experimental Design:** Three experiments (including a field experiment). Addresses endogeneity via **Random Assignment** and **Actual Behavioral Measures** (not just intentions). |
| **Canace et al. (2022)** | Disclosure & Boardroom Licensing | Disclosing conflicts of interest acts as a moral license for board members to act biasedly. | **Experimental Design:** Controlled boardroom simulations. Prevents endogeneity/bias by manipulating the timing and nature of disclosure. |
| **Bobek et al. (2024)** | Charitable Giving & Tax Compliance | Tests if charitable donations lead to tax non-compliance. Finds **consistency** instead of licensing. | **Multi-round Experiment:** 309 U.S. taxpayers. Addresses causal direction by observing decisions across multiple rounds and controlling for fairness perceptions. |
| **Lasarov & Hoffmann (2018)** | Social Moral Licensing | Group membership in a "moral" group provides a license for personal transgressions. | **Experimental Design:** Series of lab experiments. Uses **Random Allocation** to group conditions to isolate the "social" licensing effect. |
| **Gamma et al. (2018)** | Ethical Nudges & Backfire | Awareness of past hypocrisy can backfire via licensing processes. | **Field Experiment:** 1,377 consumers. Uses **Mediation Analysis** and **High/Low Construal** levels to identify boundary conditions for the effect. |
| **Joosten et al. (2013)** | Consistency vs. Compensation | Cognitive depletion leads to licensing, while available resources promote consistency. | **Experimental Design:** Two lab experiments. Addresses endogeneity by **Manipulating Cognitive Load** (depletion) to observe shifts in self-regulation. |
| **Alshehri et al. (2020)** | Spirituality & Ethical Judgment | Belief in divine forgiveness functions as a moral license to transgress. | **Quantitative/Survey:** Addresses common method bias via **Procedural Remedies** and statistical tests for social desirability. |

### Methodological Insights for v3 Integration

1.  **Addressing Endogeneity:** Most JBE papers addressing moral licensing use **Experimental Designs** to ensure random assignment and establish causality. For your **Archival Study**, the use of **Entropy Balancing** (as seen in your page 19) is a state-of-the-art alternative to the PSM and IV approaches used in Zhang & Gong (2024). You can strengthen your "Supplemental Analyses" section by noting that your entropy balancing provides comparable rigor to the experimental controls favored in JBE micro-foundations research.
2.  **Actual Behavior vs. Intentions:** Modern JBE papers (e.g., Koch-Bayram & Biemann, 2024) emphasize the importance of measuring *actual behavior* (like your discretionary accruals) rather than mere self-reported ethical intentions. This adds significant validity to your findings.
3.  **Boundary Conditions:** JBE research frequently uses **moderation analysis** (e.g., Cognitive Load in Joosten et al.; Group Status in Lasarov) to explain why licensing occurs. Your use of **Industry Culpability** as a moderator aligns perfectly with this tradition, providing a "macro" boundary condition to a "micro" psychological process.

### Key Takeaways for Manuscript Integration

*   **Self-Identity as a Buffer:** Koch-Bayram & Biemann (2024) provide a precedent for why moral licensing doesn't always occur—it's blocked by high **Environmental Self-Identity**. In your paper, you could argue that firms in **Culpable Industries** are *forced* to adopt a specific environmental identity for survival, which mimics this identity-based buffer.
*   **Cognitive and Situational Constraints:** Joosten et al. (2013) and Canace et al. (2022) suggest that licensing is most likely when actors are either cognitively depleted or lack formal accountability. This supports your argument that **Industry Culpability** (a form of extreme external accountability) suppresses the license.
*   **Archival Precedent:** Zhang & Gong (2024) provide a rare archival example in JBE, using regional regulatory pressure as a macro-level moderator similar to your use of industry-level factors.
