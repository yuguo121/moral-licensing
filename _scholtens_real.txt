ABSTRACT
We investigate how earnings management is associated with corporate social responsibility
(CSR) and investor protection with 139ﬁrms in ten Asian countries. In Asia, CSR is increasingly
attracting attention but the legal system generally is perceived as being poor. We hypothesize
that there is an inverse relationship between CSR and earnings management, as well as
between investor protection and earnings management. Regarding earnings management,
we investigate earnings smoothing and earnings aggressiveness. We ﬁnd that Asian ﬁrms
with relatively good CSR are engaged signi ﬁcantly less with earnings management. Investor
protection also is negatively associated with earnings management. We ﬁnd that this is
very case sensitive and depends on the ways in which earnings management and investor
protection are actually measured. In addition, we establish that CSR shows positive interaction
with investor protection. We conclude that in Asian countries CSR moderates ﬁrms’ earnings
management and that this is conditioned by the legal system. Copyright © 2012 John Wiley
& Sons, Ltd and ERP Environment.

Although CSR by deﬁnition is not related to ﬁnancial performance, the way in which the two affect each other has
been studied in great detail in the literature (Grif ﬁn and Mahon, 1997; McWilliams and Siegel, 2001; Margolis and
Walsh, 2003; Schuler and Cording, 2006). Margolis et al . (2007) perform a meta-analysis on 167 studies of how
CSR relates to ﬁnancial performance. They ﬁnd that in 58% of the studies, there is a non-signi ﬁcant relationship
between CSR and ﬁnancial performance. There is a positive relationship in 27% of the studies, and a negative
relationship in 2% of the studies. The other 13% did not report sample size or other key characteristics that made it
impossible to test for signi ﬁcance. This suggests that companies do not appear to suffer ﬁnancially from their CSR
policies and investments. The result will be that in equilibrium there will be no signi ﬁcant difference in the returns
of responsible investments vis-à-vis irresponsible investments by shareholders. The recent empirical evidence supports
this conjecture (Galema et al ., 2008). As to causation with ﬁnancial and social performance, Orlitzky et al .( 2 0 0 3 )
establish a bidirectional and simultaneously positive relationship between CSR and corporate ﬁnancial performance
(CFP). Hull and Rothenberg (2008) ﬁnd that innovation and the level of differentiation in the industry moderate the
positive relationship between corporate social and ﬁnancial performance. Scholtens (2008) observes that ﬁnancial
performance usually occurs before above average social performance for most CSR dimensions, whereas it is the other
way round with social and ﬁnancial risk.
Only a few empirical studies examine the relationship between CSR and earnings management. Chih et al.( 2 0 0 8 )
investigate CSR-related featuresﬁrms in 46 countries and ﬁnd that a ﬁrm with CSR in mind tends to smooth earnings
less. Furthermore, it also displays less interest in avoiding earnings losses and decreases, but it still is prone to earnings
aggressiveness. Prior et al. (2008) use a sample of 593 ﬁrms from 26 countries and demonstrate a positive impact of
earnings management on CSR. These empirical studies relate mostly to ﬁrms in western countries and refrain from
the typical Asian perspective.
Institutional stakeholder theory suggests that ﬁrms can reduce transaction and agency costs by taking social
initiatives that affect stakeholder relations (Jones, 1995). This can be either direct or indirect. The initiatives may directly
affect the stakeholder if the initiative has a positive contribution to the goals and aims of that stakeholder– for example,
if a ﬁrm which is partially owned by unions improves health and safety conditions far beyond what is legally required.
There can also be an indirect effect when the initiatives of the ﬁrm are viewed as a signal of acting in a responsible
manner. Both channels will result in fewer transaction and agency costs. As such, ﬁrms that engage in CSR will
establish relative advantages over their competitors with respect to their stakeholders. Furthermore, there is the
resource-based view of CSR (Russo and Fouts, 1997). Here, CSR actions directly impact on the stakeholder as there
is assumed to be a positive relationship between ﬁnancial and social performance. Again, there is an indirect channel
where the governance of the ﬁrm impacts on the reputation of the ﬁrm. This latter view speci ﬁcally stresses the
ﬁnancial motives of owners. Both the institutional stakeholder theory and the resource-based view of CSR depart from
the perspective of the ﬁrm. They point out why the ﬁrm might want to engage with CSR in connection with its owners
and other stakeholders. Businesses which stress their social responsibility tend to signal that they are‘good companies’.
Therefore, their behavior regarding earnings management may differ from other businesses.

agency problems (Stein and Roseﬁelde, 2005). La Porta et al. (1998) suggest this in part results from the legal system.

industrialized countries. The World Bank (http://www.worldbank.org/prospects/gep2000/), characterizes the

Hypotheses
The review above shows that there are several studies that investigate the connection between CSR and earnings
management. Earnings management is considered to be an agency cost because managers pursue their own
interests by making up ﬁnancial reports that do not express the real economic situation of the ﬁrm (Shapiro,
2005; Prior et al ., 2008). Some researchers emphasize particular ways to respond to the agency problems. Fama
and Jensen (1983) and Roe (1991) suggest an increase in monitoring effectiveness by independent corporate boards.
Luoma and Goodstein (1999) and Prior et al . (2008) argue it is helpful to reinforce the ﬁrm’s perceived social
legitimacy. Alternatively, the ﬁrms might improve their CSR (Liu and Lu, 2007; Huang et al., 2008). The latter does
seem to happen indeed: several researchers ﬁnd that ﬁrms use CSR to reduce con ﬂicts of interest between
managers and stakeholders (Jensen, 2001; Calton and Payne, 2003; Scherer et al ., 2006; Harjoto and Jo, 2011).
Here, the main idea is that CSR can mitigate agency problems, especially the agency con ﬂicts between controlling
shareholders and minority shareholders. As a consequence, CSR may reduce the incentives to manage earnings. On
the basis of the discussion in the literature, we want to test the hypothesis that earnings management is negatively
related to CSR:
H1: Corporate social responsibility is inversely related to earnings management.
In addition, La Porta et al . (1998) ﬁnd that countries with strong legal protection of investors have more
developed stock markets, better corporate governance, and higher ﬁrm valuation than countries with weak legal
(La Porta et al ., 1998; Denis and McCommell, 2003). Leuz et al . (2003) examine whether earnings management
varies across countries that differ with respect to investor protection. They ﬁnd that earnings management occurs
less frequently in countries where outside investors have more protection from the country ’s legal system. Welford
(2007) argues that ownership and control of many ﬁrms in Asia differ from those commonly seen in the West. He
especially stresses the fact that many Asian companies are dominated by controlling shareholders. This suggests
that investor protection and corporate governance would need to be even stronger in the Asian region than
elsewhere. We want to test the hypothesis that the earnings management is negatively related to investor protection:
H2: Investor protection is inversely related to earnings management.
The null hypothesis in both instances is that there is no signi ﬁcant relationship between earnings management
and CSR and between earnings management and investor protection in Asia respectively.

Earnings Management
We use different indicators of earnings management. As such, we want to test the robustness of our approach
regarding the assessment of earnings management in Asia. We will investigate two different measures of earnings
management, namely earnings smoothing and earnings aggressiveness. Both measures are used a lot in the empirical
studies on earnings management (Bernhardt and Campello, 2007; Jo and Kim, 2007; Chih et al., 2008; Gong et al.,
2008). Earnings smoothing relates to concealing shocks to theﬁrm’so p e r a t i n gc a s hﬂows (Leuz et al., 2003). Earnings
aggressiveness has a different focus as it relates to misstating the actual performance of the ﬁrm (Bhattacharya et al.,
2003). Thus, instead of relying on one single measure of earnings management, we will provide several alternatives.
In the ﬁrst place, this is in line with the nuanced discussion about what earnings management actually is (see
introduction and background section). Secondly, it provides us with a sound idea about the robustness of our results
for different measures of earnings management.
Earnings Smoothing
First, we use two different instruments for earnings smoothing. The ﬁrst instrument (EM1) concentrates on the
manipulation of earnings in relation to the cash ﬂows of the ﬁrm. With this ﬁrst measure of earnings smoothing,

accounting earnings are the sum of accruals and cashﬂows from operations,ﬁrms may increase the reporting of future
revenues or they may delay the reporting of costs to hide poor current performance. Alternatively, insiders may also
underreport strong performance to create reserves for the future.

Model and Methodology
As we want to ﬁnd out what drives earnings management, we depart from the model by Chih et al . (2008). They
hypothesize that the relationship between CSR and earnings management can be negative, positive, or neutral
and investigate mostly industrialized economies. In our study, we limit ourselves to Asian countries and argue that
differences in investor protection might play a conditioning role. Therefore, we will basically depart from Chih et al.
(2008) but will account for interaction with investor protection. Another important differences is that they measure
CSR at the country level by relating the number of listed ﬁrms that are included in sustainability index FTSE4Good
Global to the total number of listed ﬁrms in that country. In contrast, we analyze CSR at the level of the individual
ﬁrm and then report our results at the country level.
We investigate the following aspects: CSR, ﬁrm size, ﬁnancial performance, and leverage as ﬁrm-speciﬁc variables,
and per capita GDP, in ﬂation, legal enforcement and anti-director rights as the country-speci ﬁc variables. Legal
enforcement and anti-director rights are two variables that both re ﬂect the degree of investor protection in a country.

As discussed above, we hypothesize that the extent of earnings management is inversely related to the extent of CSR.
We regard earnings management as an act of irresponsibility and as inconsistent with appropriate CSR. Furthermore,
we expect that the relationship between the two investor protection variables, legal enforcement and anti-director rights,
and earnings management is negative; this is our second hypothesis. With stronger rights for stakeholders and
shareholders, the management of the ﬁrm will encounter more dif ﬁculties in managing earnings (La Porta et al .,
1998). This is conﬁrmed by Leuz et al. (2003) who ﬁnd that earnings management occurs less frequently in countries
where outside investors are more protected.
The relationship between ﬁrm size and earnings management is not clear beforehand. Dechow and Dichev (2002)
show that larger ﬁrms tend to have more stable and predictable operations and hence report smaller amounts of
discretionary accruals (Kim and Yi, 2006). Thus, larger ﬁrms appear to have an incentive to engage in more earnings
management. However, at the same time, largerﬁrms usually also are prone to closer scrutiny by outsiders and are re-
quired to disclose their information more often. Hence, it is more difﬁcult for larger ﬁrms to manage earnings (Lee and
Choi, 2002; Chih et al., 2008). Given these con ﬂicting ap r i o r iperspectives about the role of ﬁrm size in connection
with earnings management, their ultimate relationship will have to be decided on the basis of the data.
Prior studies ﬁnd that earnings management is more prevalent in poorly performing ﬁrms (Kothari et al., 2005;
Cornett et al ., 2008). This suggests that these ﬁrms manage their income in an opportunistic manner to create
distortions in the reported earnings. Therefore, we assume that the relationship between leverage and earnings
management is positive. This is because ﬁrms which are subject to more risk (as re ﬂected in higher leverage) will be
inclined to manage their earnings more than less risky ﬁrms. For example, Kim and Yi (2006) ﬁnd that Korean ﬁrms
with high leverage engage more in earnings management than ﬁrms with lower leverage.
With respect to the role of country-level variables, we arrive at the following notions about their relationship with
earnings management. We expect that per capita GDP will be negatively related to earnings management. In more
developed countries, it will be less likely and less accepted that ﬁrms manage earnings (Chih et al., 2008). The relation
between inﬂation and earnings management may be positive because a rise in the price level makes it more difﬁcult for
the public and the analysts to see through the ‘veil’ of money.
Data
Although CSR is often regarded as a universal concept, one should not neglect the fact that the actual meaning of
CSR may differ between countries and regions due to varying socio-political and cultural circumstances (Moon,
2007; Steurer and Konrad, 2009). In addition, CSR can be measured in different ways (Wood, 2010). One approach
is the balanced scorecard (see Hansen et al. (2010) for an application with regards to Merck in Thailand). However,
if the aim is to compare a large number of ﬁrms, this approach is not very practical and using CSR ratings is an
alternative. Another approach is to check whether ﬁrms are included in sustainability indexes. Chih et al . (2008)
use this indicator. However, then CSR is a yes/no variable. This contrasts with the literature on CSR that suggests
it rather is a matter of degree (Chatterji et al., 2009). Therefore, we will want to use ﬁrm-speciﬁc data from ratings
agency. However, this type of information may be imperfect as well (Chatterji et al., 2009). As a consequence, the
results will have to be interpreted with caution.

Conclusion
This paper examines the interrelationships between CSR, investor protection, and earnings management for 139ﬁrms
in ten Asian countries. In general, we establish that theseﬁrms’ CSR is inversely related to earnings management. This
suggests that ﬁrms that perform better on CSR are engaged signi ﬁcantly less in earnings management. This ﬁnding
relates to different ways of measuring earnings management. If we relate earnings management to investor protection,
a somewhat mixed pattern emerges. We ﬁnd that with one speciﬁc indicator of earnings management (EM1), it is legal
enforcement that is inversely related to this earnings management and also signi ﬁcantly conditions CSR. But, in this
case, anti-director rights do not show a relationship with earnings management, directly or indirectly by conditioning
CSR. For the other two indicators of earnings management (EM2 and EM3), it exactly is the other way round: Only anti-
director rights is inversely related to earnings management and is a signiﬁcant conditioning factor with respect to CSR.
Thus, to some extent, law enforcement and anti-director rights appear to act as substitutes. These ﬁndings are in line
with the results from previous studies by Chih et al . (2008) and Prior et al. (2008) on geographically more diverse
samples. Furthermore, they are consistent with the country studies of Kim and Yi (2006) for Korea, Liu and Lu
(2007) for China, and Charoenwong and Jiraporn (2009) for Singapore and Thailand. We add value to the literature
by focusing on Asian ﬁrms in ten countries.
We also ﬁnd that larger ﬁrms are engaged less in earnings smoothing than smaller ﬁrms. This is consistent with
the ﬁndings of Kothari et al . (2005) and Cornett et al . (2008). Furthermore, ﬁrms with superior ﬁnancial perfor-
mance engage less earnings smoothing, but they do tend to show earnings aggressiveness. These ﬁndings are in
line with the results of Leuz et al . (2003), Jaggi and Tsui (2007), and Königsgruber (2009). Regarding the coun-
try-level variables, the results for the control variables appear to be sensitive to the way in which earnings manage-
ment is measured. For example, ﬁrms in high-income and low-in ﬂation countries tend to be engaged less in
earnings smoothing but show more earnings aggressiveness. Another important ﬁnding is that Asian ﬁrms in
countries with stronger legal enforcement engage in less earnings smoothing (EM1) and this negative effect will
increase when the ﬁrms have higher CSR scores. These ﬁndings are consistent with the study by Leuz et al .
(2003) for ﬁrms that are mostly located in industrialized economies and complement the ﬁndings of Baughn
et al . (2007) and Welford (2007) for Asia.
The results suggest that CSR is related to ‘real’ actions of the ﬁrm and that it can have an impact. This impact is
usually conditioned by both ﬁrm and country characteristics. Financial performance and investor protection are the
most important conditions in this respect. The ﬁ
ndings provide an empirical basis for institutional stakeholder theory
which suggests that ﬁrms can reduce the transaction costs by taking social initiatives that connect with stakeholders
(Jones, 1995). Furthermore, it con ﬁrms the resource-based view of CSR, which holds that CSR action impact on the
stakeholder because of the positive relationship between ﬁnancial and social performance (Russo and Fouts, 1997).
We infer several important policy recommendations from our analysis. First, from the perspective of the business
community, it suggests that investing in CSR can be beneﬁcial for reducing earnings management. Thus, shareholders
have a case in advancing CSR. However, CSR is not a suf ﬁcient condition to get rid of earnings management.
Additional effort will be needed. Second, from a public policy perspective, our results suggest that the efforts to advance
investor protection are useful. However, they turn out to be very speci ﬁc regarding how earnings management is
actually measured. The combination of anti-director rights and law enforcement is important in this respect. Earnings
management damages the business climate and, as such, economic and social development. Our study helps
policymakers in building their case to confront directors on earnings management. A further policy recommendation
is that there is a case for both the business community and policy makers to promote the reporting of CSR
performance.
We want to mention some limitations regarding our study. First is that our data sample is limited. This especially is
due to the lack of extensive CSR information for Asian ﬁrms. As such, our results need to be regarded as preliminary
ﬁndings. Second, and relatedly, the data from ASR is backward looking. As such, our results cannot be used to predict
any future relationships regarding CSR, investor protection and earnings management. Third is that with investor
protection we ﬁnd that our results are very much dependent on the way in which earnings management is calculated.
Furthermore, the data limitations restrict us to using rather straightforward estimation techniques. More detailedﬁrm-
speciﬁc information about Asian companies would allow us to use more advanced techniques. This could provide us
with a more thorough understanding of the complexities involved.

Appendix A. A brief overview of the expectations for each of the categories
1. Governance, Codes, and Policies
The indicators in this section assess the availability and communication of company policies and codes of conduct in
relation to key CSR factors including corporate governance, risk management, anti-corruption, and labor and
human rights issues.
2. CSR Strategy and Communication
The indicators in this section assess company strategy on CSR and how activities are communicated to stakeholders
through reporting initiatives such as the use of internationally recognized reporting guidelines, stakeholder
engagement programmes, CSR training and awareness and alignment with voluntary CSR standards in company
operations.
3. Marketplace and Supply Chain
The indicators in this section assess the supply, delivery and distribution of products and services and customer
focused activities. This includes the areas of health and safety management, supply chain standards and supplier
engagement.
4. Workplace and People
The indicators in this section assess how a company treats its employees and other community stakeholders and
how their actions are communicated both internally and externally, this includes health policies and human
resource issues, training and lifelong learning, diversity and freedom of association.
5. Environment
The indicators in this section assess the level of environmental data and targets set by the company and how they
are reported to stakeholders. Indicators include environmental management systems, emissions data, the use of
renewable energy and customer and employee focused environmental initiatives.
6. Community and Development
Community investment initiatives are perhaps the most well-known aspects of CSR in an Asia speci ﬁcc o n t e x t
with its rich history of philanthropy. However, the emerging model for issues in this section are how community
initiatives are reported on and if there is evidence of data collection and monitoring of impacts and longer term
goals. The indicators in this section assess evidence of long term strategies and targets, monitoring systems and
quantiﬁable impacts of the investment and related employee volunteering.
